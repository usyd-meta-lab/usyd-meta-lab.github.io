Guidance

Why this flow matters

Many research projects take longer than we first expect. In our experience, it is not unusual for a project to take around two years from the first idea to publication. Part of growing as a researcher is learning how to navigate that timeline efficiently. The steps shown in the flowchart represent the main stages of a project: moving more smoothly through them is one of the most effective ways to increase productivity.

A key principle is that we are not aiming simply to write up experiments that happen to “work.” Instead, we want to design studies that can be replicated, withstand scrutiny, and genuinely contribute to cumulative science. Efficiency here does not mean cutting corners, but rather being intentional about where time and resources are invested.

One strategy is to think in terms of a minimum viable experiment (MVE)—a research analogue of the “minimum viable product” used in technology and business. The MVE is the simplest, most streamlined version of an experiment that still provides a valid test of a research question.

  • If the MVE fails, then little time or resources have been wasted, and we have learned that the idea or design needs revision.
  • If the MVE succeeds, then it provides immediate information, gives confidence in the research direction, and highlights which design features should be scaled up or refined.

Just as in product development, the MVE mindset encourages us to:

  • Test quickly and iterate rather than investing heavily before the concept is validated.
  • Learn from small-scale pilots to identify potential flaws or strengths.
  • Focus on the “core feature”—the critical manipulation or hypothesis—before adding complexity.
  • Scale only after validation, ensuring that later, more resource-intensive experiments build on a solid foundation.

By adopting this approach, we can spend less time chasing unproductive avenues, build more robust projects, and contribute to a culture of replication and cumulative progress rather than one-off findings.

How to use this flow

The diagram reflects our typical research lifecycle. Use it to plan projects, communicate status to collaborators, and decide when to iterate or stop.

Design Experiment

  • Research question and falsifiable hypotheses.
  • Design: task, IVs/DVs, primary outcome.
  • Sample: size (a priori power), source, inclusion/exclusion.
  • Analysis plan: primary test, covariates, stopping rules, blinding.
  • Approvals: ethics, data handling, preregistration draft.

Code Experiment

  • Implement task (jsPsych v8), versioned in GitHub.
  • Automated DataPipe logging; pilot locally and on host.
  • Device checks, accessibility, performance budget.
  • README with parameters and usage.

Collect Data

  • Recruitment (SONA/Prolific) with clear screening.
  • Monitor dropout, attention checks, data quality dashboards.
  • Secure storage

Analyse

  • Reproducible script/notebook; prereg vs. exploratory split.
  • Assumption checks; model diagnostics; effect sizes & CIs.
  • Sensitivity/power updates based on observed variance.

Decision

  • Collect more data: power not met or precision inadequate.
  • Stop project: criteria unmet or pivot justified.
  • Proceed to write up: criteria met; archive analysis.

Write Up

  • Transparent Methods, prereg links, materials on OSF.
  • Share code/data (as permissible), report limitations.
  • Prepare figures from the analysis pipeline for traceability.

Pre‑registration checklist

  • Primary outcomes and exclusion criteria defined a priori.
  • Stopping rules (n or time) documented.
  • Randomisation/Counterbalancing scheme described.
  • Planned analyses with model specification.
